# Economics
*Courses: Microeconomic Analysis (MECN 430) · Business Analytics · Finance II*

> Use this when analyzing market structure, making cost-based decisions, applying game theory to competitive dynamics, or thinking about pricing logic at the market level.

---

## Quick Reference

- **Opportunity cost** — always the right cost for any decision; what are you giving up?
- **Relevant cost principle** — only costs that *change* with the decision matter
- **Market structures** — Perfect competition → Monopolistic → Oligopoly → Monopoly; structure determines strategy
- **Nash equilibrium** — each player's strategy is the best response to all others; no one can unilaterally improve
- **Consumer/producer surplus** — value creation is split; markets maximize total surplus (but not equally)
- **Externalities** — costs/benefits not reflected in price; create market failures

---

## Frameworks

### Cost Fundamentals
- **Opportunity cost** — value of the best alternative forgone; relevant both before a decision (how else to spend) and after (how else to use an asset)
- **Relevant cost principle** — only include costs whose level is affected by the decision (sunk costs are irrelevant)
- **Fixed cost (FC)** — does not change with volume
- **Variable cost (VC)** — changes with volume
- **Marginal cost (MC)** — cost of one additional unit; the true cost at the margin
- **Average total cost (ATC)** = TC / Q; distinguish from MC (MC below ATC → ATC falling; MC above → ATC rising)

**Alcoa case:** Built an aluminum smelter in Iceland despite no raw ore — because low energy costs dominated the relevant cost calculation. Location decision driven entirely by the right cost.

---

### Market Structures

| Structure | Players | Price Power | Example |
|-----------|---------|-------------|---------|
| Perfect competition | Many | None (price-taker) | Aluminum, agriculture |
| Monopolistic competition | Many | Some | Restaurants, apparel |
| Oligopoly | Few | Strategic interaction | Airlines, telecom |
| Monopoly | One | Full | Regulated utilities |

Structure determines the right strategy. In perfect competition, cost position is everything. In monopoly/oligopoly, pricing power and entry barriers are the game.

**Brewery example:** 1873 = 4,000 US breweries. 1985 = 40 (economies of scale). 2024 = ~10,000 (microbreweries — rising income + home brewing legalization lowered entry barriers). Market structure is not static.

### Supply & Demand
- **Consumer surplus** = WTP − Price (area above the price line, below the demand curve)
- **Producer surplus** = Price − Cost (area below the price line, above the supply curve)
- **Deadweight loss** = surplus destroyed by monopoly pricing, taxes, or market failures
- **Fundamental theorem of capitalism:** Free markets maximize total surplus — but not its distribution

---

### Game Theory & Oligopoly
- **Nash equilibrium** — each player's strategy is the best response to the others'; no one can unilaterally improve
- **Prisoner's dilemma** — individually rational choices produce collectively bad outcomes (e.g., price wars, advertising arms races)
- **Commitment devices** — make a credible threat or promise by removing your own flexibility (burning the boats)
- **First-mover advantage** — valuable only when the first mover can commit to a position the follower can't profitably undercut
- **Dominant strategy** — best regardless of what the other player does; if it exists, play it

---

### Externalities & Market Failures
- **Negative externality** — costs imposed on third parties not in the price (pollution, congestion, spam email)
- **Positive externality** — benefits to third parties not captured by the producer (education, vaccination, R&D)
- Corrections: Pigouvian taxes/subsidies · Regulation · Property rights (Coase theorem — if rights are clear and transactions are cheap, parties bargain to the efficient outcome)

---

### Economies of Scale & Scope
- **Economies of scale** — unit cost falls as volume increases; drives industry consolidation
- **Economies of scope** — cost savings from producing multiple products together; drives diversification
- **Diseconomies of scale** — unit cost rises at very large scale; coordination costs, bureaucracy

**Queuing corollary (see also 7_operations):** Larger pooled service systems have lower wait times per customer at the same utilization. Economies of scale in queuing.

---

### Pricing Logic (Market Level)
- **Price-taker** — takes market price as given; compete on cost
- **Price-setter** — has market power; set price where MR = MC
- **Price discrimination** — charge different prices to different segments; maximizes producer surplus capture
- **Two-part tariff** — fixed fee + per-unit charge (gym membership + per-class fee)
- **Bundling** — sell products together; extracts more surplus when WTP is negatively correlated across products

---

## Mental Models

**Opportunity cost is always the right cost.** The sticker price of a decision is rarely the full cost. Before any choice, ask: what's the value of what I'm giving up?

**Market structure is strategy context.** The right move in a commodity market is completely different from the right move in an oligopoly. Identify the structure before you recommend a strategy.

**Equilibrium is a tendency, not a guarantee.** Markets move toward equilibrium — but slowly, with friction, and interrupted by new entrants, regulation, and technology. The interesting opportunities live in the gaps before equilibrium is restored.
