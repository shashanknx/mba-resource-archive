## Frameworks

### Porter's Five Forces
*Use when:* Evaluating an industry or a company's position within it.

1. **Threat of new entrants** — barriers: capital, regulation, brand, scale, switching costs
2. **Supplier power** — concentrated suppliers, few substitutes = strong power
3. **Buyer power** — concentrated buyers, commoditized product = strong power
4. **Threat of substitutes** — products from a *different* industry doing the same job
5. **Rivalry** — growth rate, differentiation, exit barriers, # of players

All five determine long-run industry profitability — not any one company's performance.

---

### Value Creation & Capture
- **Value Created** = Benefit (B) − Cost (C)
- **Consumer Surplus** = B − Price (what buyers keep)
- **Profit** = Price − C (what you keep)
- **Added Value** = coalition value *with* you − coalition value *without* you

To capture more: be irreplaceable. To create more: raise B, lower C, or increase quantity.

---

### AAA Framework — Assets → Activities → Advantage
- What resources do I have that others don't?
- What activities do I do differently or better?
- What advantage does this create, and is it sustainable?

Ask: Can competitors imitate this cheaply? Can substitutes erode it? Will customers still value it in 5 years?

---

### Competitive Advantage Modes
- **Cost leadership** — same product at lower cost; requires scale, process discipline
- **Differentiation** — unique value customers pay a premium for; requires brand, innovation, intimacy
- **Stuck in the middle** = no clear advantage = poor performance (Porter's warning)

---

### Impact Investing (SAN Ventures Framework)
**Investment thesis structure:**
1. Why this sector? (structural tailwind, timing, impact thesis)
2. Why early-stage? (information edge, entry price, relationship capital)
3. What do we look for? (founder profile, traction signals, model, impact metrics)
4. What do we avoid? (red flags, structural deal-breakers)

**Deal review structure:**
1. Solution & Value Proposition
2. Merits (traction, differentiation, team, unit economics)
3. Key Concerns (regulatory, mission-monetization tension, scale ceiling)
4. Top 3–5 Questions for management

**5 Ps of Impact:** Place · People · Process · Product · Paradigm

**Startup archetypes:** Unicorn (growth at all costs) · Cockroach (survive anything) · Camel (balance growth + cash) · Zebra (profitable + improves society)

## Cases & Examples

| Case | Lesson |
|------|--------|
| Enterprise Rent-A-Car | Won by serving a neglected segment (insurance replacement) incumbents ignored |
| Disney | IP flywheel — one character monetizes across films, parks, merchandise, licensing |
| MoviePass | Negative unit economics + no structural moat = inevitable failure |
| Starbucks | Growth diluted the core differentiator ("third place"); scale can erode strategy |
| CF Industries | Commodity market → cost position is everything; be the low-cost producer closest to the customer |
| PadSplit | Strong unit economics killed by regulatory risk (innkeeper's law); always identify the one thing that kills this |

## Mental Models

**Strategy requires trade-offs.** A strategy that tries to be everything is a wish list. The best strategies are defined as much by what they *don't* do.

**"Establish a difference that can be preserved."** Temporary advantage is just a price war waiting to happen.

**Impact and financial incentives must be structurally aligned.** If the business model rewards compromising impact, it eventually will.
