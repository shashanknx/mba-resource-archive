# Marketing

## Quick Reference

- **STP** — Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning; the foundation of any marketing strategy
- **EVC** — Economic Value to Customer; sets your pricing ceiling vs. a reference alternative
- **Rational Buyer model** — customers buy when their surplus from you > surplus from the alternative
- **Margin math** — Unit contribution = Revenue/unit − Variable cost/unit; Break-even = FC / Unit contribution
- **Paths to growth** — Penetration · New to category · Usage/Upsell · New segments
- **Backward research design** — start with the decision, not the method

## Frameworks

### STP — Segmentation · Targeting · Positioning

**Segmentation:** Divide the market into groups with meaningfully different needs.
Bases: Demographic · Geographic · Psychographic · Behavioral (usage, loyalty, benefits sought)
Good segments are: measurable, substantial, accessible, differentiable, actionable.

**Targeting:** Choose which segment(s) to serve. Evaluate on: size, growth rate, competitive intensity, fit with your capabilities.

**Positioning:** How you want to be perceived in your target segment's mind.
> *"For [segment], [brand] is the [frame of reference] that [POD], because [RTB]."*

*"A product for everyone rarely reaches anyone."* The goal is to be the obvious choice for a specific segment, not the adequate choice for everyone.

### Paths to Growth
- **Penetration** — steal share from competitors (differentiation or price)
- **New to category** — bring in non-users entirely (e.g., Uber vs. car ownership)
- **Usage/Upsell** — increase how much existing customers buy
- **New segments** — expand to adjacent demographics, geographies, or use cases

---

### Pricing Strategy

**Rational Buyer Framework:**
- **Reservation Price (RP)** = max willingness to pay
- **Surplus** = RP − Price; buyers maximize surplus
- Customer buys from you when: Surplus(you) ≥ Surplus(alternative)

**EVC — Economic Value to Customer:**
*EVC = Price of reference product + Incremental benefit of your product*
Sets your pricing ceiling. Focus only on the incremental value over the next best alternative.

**The margin rule:** Pricing is a gamble between margin and volume. A 1% price increase beats a 1% cost decrease because volume increases also increase variable costs. Price is the highest-leverage lever in the P&L.

**Price discrimination:**
- 1st degree — charge each customer their exact RP (theoretical)
- 2nd degree — versioning/quantity (tiered plans, volume discounts)
- 3rd degree — segment-based (student discounts, geography)

**Key rule:** To capture value, align *what you sell* with *what creates the value*. Pricing by dose only makes sense if buyers know dosage in advance.

---

### Marketing Math
- **Margin** = (Price − Cost) / Price (% of retail)
- **Markup** = (Price − Wholesale) / Wholesale (% of wholesale)
- **Unit contribution** = Revenue/unit − Variable cost/unit
- **Break-even** = Fixed Costs / Unit Contribution
- **Profit** = (Units × Unit Contribution) − Fixed Costs

**Retailer dynamics:** Dropping your price lowers the dollar margin retailers earn → less incentive for them to push your product. % margin vs. unit margin can move in opposite directions.

### Research Design

**Types of data:**
- **Generated data** (reviews, usage logs) — high volume; watch for selection bias
- **Experiments** (A/B tests) — reveals causal effect on behavior
- **Surveys** — stated preference; people underreport sensitive behaviors

**Backward research design:**
1. What decision does the manager need to make?
2. What does the final report need to show?
3. What analyses populate that report?
4. What data is needed?

Start with the decision. Design backward. Surprising conclusions should be anticipated, not discovered.

**Survivorship bias:** You only see the planes that came back. The most diagnostic data is often what's missing. Always ask: what am I not seeing, and why?

**Conjoint analysis:** Present hypothetical product bundles varying on attributes. Model which attributes drive choice and the dollar value of each level (part-worth utilities). Use for: pricing decisions, feature prioritization, new product design.

## Cases & Examples

| Case | Lesson |
|------|--------|
| Datril vs. Tylenol | Price as a quality signal; dropping price can undermine your brand and retailer support simultaneously |
| Angiomax | Align the pricing unit with the value creation unit; educating buyers helps only when it lets them self-sort into higher-value options |
| Swiffer in Canada | Your value prop is what the customer hears, not what you say; repositioned from "replaces mopping" to "top-off cleaner" |
| Gatorade Light | Poor product-market fit; wrong segment, wrong benefits, wrong positioning |

## Mental Models

**Value creation ≠ value capture.** Creating value doesn't mean you'll keep it. Appropriability (IP, switching costs, complementary assets) determines how much you actually capture. See 1_design_innovation for Teece framework.

**"People don't want to feel like the sense of fairness is broken."** Even optimal price discrimination creates backlash when customers discover it. Price strategically; watch for trust erosion.
