# Design & Innovation
*Courses: Appropriability by Design · Innovation Strategy · Research Design Build · Growth Innovation*

> Use this when building or evaluating a new product, understanding users, or thinking about how to protect and capture value from an innovation.

## Frameworks

### DVF — Desirability · Viability · Feasibility
The sweet spot is the intersection of all three.
- **Desirability** — Do real customers want this? Will they pay?
- **Viability** — Does the business model work?
- **Feasibility** — Can you actually build and deliver it?

A desirable + feasible idea with no viable model is a charity. A viable + feasible idea nobody wants is a solution in search of a problem.

---

### JTBD — Jobs-To-Be-Done
People hire products to do a job — functional, emotional, or social. Understand the job, not the demographic.

**Warby Parker:** The job wasn't "buy glasses." It was "feel smart and stylish without paying a premium tax." They competed against an industry assumption, not another eyewear brand.

**JTBD interview questions:**
- What were you doing right before you looked for this solution?
- What have you tried before? What frustrated you?
- What would you do if this didn't exist?
- What does success look like in 6 months?

---

### HMW — How Might We
Reframe problems before generating solutions. Format: *"How might we [verb] [subject] so that [outcome]?"*

Too narrow = constrains solutions. Too broad = no direction. Find the level of abstraction that opens creative space without losing focus.

**Step / Stretch / Leap:** Generate concepts at three levels — incremental improvement, analogous solution from another domain, radical reimagining.

---

### AEIOU — Field Research
Five lenses for observing users in their actual context:
- **A**ctivities — what are they doing and trying to accomplish?
- **E**nvironments — where are they? What's the setting?
- **I**nteractions — how do they interact with objects, systems, people?
- **O**bjects — what tools and artifacts are they using?
- **U**sers — who are they? What are their roles and values?

Observation → Patterns → Insights → HMWs. An observation is "users refresh the page repeatedly." An insight is "users don't trust the system's status indicators, so they create their own."

### Business Model Canvas (9 Blocks)
| Block | Key Question |
|-------|-------------|
| Customer Segments | Who are we creating value for? |
| Value Propositions | What problem do we solve? |
| Channels | How do we reach customers? |
| Customer Relationships | What relationship does each segment expect? |
| Revenue Streams | How do we make money? |
| Key Resources | What assets does the VP require? |
| Key Activities | What must we do to deliver the VP? |
| Key Partnerships | Who helps us, and why? |
| Cost Structure | What are our major costs? |

### Appropriability — Profiting from Innovation (Teece)
Two variables determine whether you capture the value you create:
1. **Appropriability regime** — How strong is IP protection? How easy is imitation?
2. **Complementary assets** — Do you need to control manufacturing, distribution, or brand to commercialize?

**IP types at a glance:**
- **Patents** — must be new, useful, non-obvious, enabled. Expensive. File PCT for international protection.
- **Trade secrets** — no disclosure required; last forever if protected. Best for processes.
- **Trademarks** — source and expression layer; deeper moat than patents once established.
- **Copyright** — original works in any tangible medium.

**The Sandwich:** Innovators get squeezed between IP holders above and manufacturers below. Escape by owning the IP *and* complementary assets, or by commoditizing one layer to capture value in another.

### Innovation Postures (3M Framework)
- **Core (70%)** — sustaining improvements to existing products and markets
- **Adjacent (20%)** — new uses for existing capabilities, nearby markets
- **Transformational (10%)** — new category creation, disruption

Don't starve the core. Don't neglect transformation and get disrupted.

---

### Liquid Expectations
Customers compare their experience with you to the best experience they've had *anywhere* — not just in your category. Netflix sets hospitality expectations. Amazon sets B2B purchasing expectations.

Three competitor types: **Direct** (same category), **Experiential** (replaces the need for you), **Perceptual** (shapes what customers expect).

## Mental Models

**Invention ≠ Innovation.** Innovation = creation *and* capture of new value. You can invent something and have someone else profit from it entirely.

**The job is the unit of analysis.** Not the product, not the persona — the job the customer is trying to get done. Everything follows from understanding that.
